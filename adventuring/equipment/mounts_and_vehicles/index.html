Mounts and Vehicles

A good mount can help you move more quickly through the wilderness, but its primary purpose is to carry the gear that would otherwise slow you down. The Mounts and Other Animals table shows each animal’s speed and base carrying capacity.
An animal pulling a carriage, cart, chariot, sled, or wagon can move weight up to five times its base carrying capacity, including the weight of the vehicle. If multiple animals pull the same vehicle, they can add their carrying capacity together.
Mounts other than those listed here are available in fantasy gaming worlds, but they are rare and not normally available for purchase. These include flying mounts (pegasi, griffons, hippogriffs, and similar animals) and even aquatic mounts (giant sea horses, for example). Acquiring such a mount often means securing an egg and raising the creature yourself, making a bargain with a powerful entity, or negotiating with the mount itself.
Barding. Barding is armor designed to protect an animal’s head, neck, chest, and body. Any type of armor shown on the Armor table can be purchased as barding. The cost is four times the equivalent armor made for humanoids, and it weighs twice as much.
Saddles. A military saddle braces the rider, helping you keep your seat on an active mount in battle. It gives you advantage on any check you make to remain mounted. An exotic saddle is required for riding any aquatic or flying mount.
Vehicle Proficiency. If you have proficiency with a certain kind of vehicle (land or water), you can add your proficiency bonus to any check you make to control that kind of vehicle in difficult circumstances.
Rowed Vessels. Keelboats and rowboats are used on lakes and rivers. If going downstream, add the speed of the current (typically 3 miles per hour) to the speed of the vehicle. These vehicles can’t be rowed against any significant current, but they can be pulled upstream by draft animals on the shores. A rowboat weighs 100 pounds, in case adventurers carry it over land.
Land Vehicles. Powered land vehicles offer distinct advantages in terms of speed, but they are often not appropriate for unpaved surfaces. Land vehicles tend to have higher upkeep costs and may require special training to use effectively. Additionally, some vehicles like hovercars require special housings and landing pads when not in use.
Air Vehicles. Air vehicles are built to be the fastest form of atmospheric transportation available. However, they require constant maintenance, extensive training, and almost all require an appropriate landing surface -- ideally in the form of a runway. The larger the air vehicle, the longer the runway required, with the longest runways being about a mile long.
Space Vehicles. Space vehicles come in a variety of shapes and sizes, including one-time-use rockets, freighters, and even warships. Space vehicles are capable of moving at a variety of speeds, including the necessary speed to achieve orbit from the planet upon which they were constructed. Long-range space vehicles come equipped with Star Drives which let them traverse the space between star systems by folding spacetime. Star Drives come in four levels, each corresponding to the number of star systems they're able to visit before needing to recharge for at least 24 hours.
